# Content Strategy

This document defines the content strategy for the MVP portfolio page. It describes what each section should communicate, what user question it answers, and what information must support the story.

It does not define visual design, layout, component structure, or wireframes.

## Task

TASK-005 - Content Strategy

Goal: define the future site content as information architecture for each MVP section.

The content must build a professional story instead of listing technologies. Each section must answer one primary user question and move the viewer to the next stage of trust.

## Source Context

This strategy is based on:

- Product Vision from `docs/planning/MCP.md`;
- Experience Architecture from `docs/planning/INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md`;
- Design Bible from `docs/design/DESIGN_BIBLE.md`;
- Theme Concept from `docs/design/PROJECT_IDENTITY.md`.

## Content Principles

- Tell one story: product-minded frontend developer with engineering discipline.
- Keep every section focused on one user question.
- Use technologies only as evidence, not as the main content.
- Write for recruiters first, with enough technical substance for engineering reviewers.
- Avoid resume language, inflated claims, and generic portfolio cliches.
- Let the AI section provide optional depth instead of making the main page dense.
- Keep `Ask my AI` as the primary CTA only in the final AI section.

## Full Content Structure

### 1. Hero

Purpose: create immediate professional clarity and emotional interest.

Primary user question:

> Who is this developer, and why should I keep reading?

Main heading direction:

- A concise positioning statement about frontend development, product clarity, and engineering quality.
- The heading should describe professional value, not a job search slogan.

Subtitle direction:

- One short paragraph explaining that the developer builds clear, maintainable interfaces and works with product intent, architecture, accessibility, and AI-assisted engineering ownership.

Key message:

- This is a Frontend Developer who treats frontend as product engineering, not only screen assembly.

Additional text:

- Short identity line with name and role.
- Optional availability or focus label if it helps recruiters scan quickly.
- No biography, no long stack list, no dense proof.

Supporting elements:

- Professional photo as a human trust signal.
- Role label: Frontend Developer.
- Short value markers, for example product clarity, maintainable UI, accessibility, responsible AI workflow.
- Primary page CTA: `See how I work`.
- Optional secondary CTA: `View experience`.

What the user should understand:

- The candidate is a frontend developer with product and engineering judgment.
- The site is intentional and not a generic resume export.
- There is a reason to continue reading.

### 2. What I Can Bring to a Team

Purpose: translate first impression into practical team value.

Primary user question:

> What will this person improve for our team?

Main heading direction:

- A direct promise about team contribution, not a list of skills.

Subtitle direction:

- A short explanation that her work is focused on clarity, maintainability, collaboration, and polished user-facing implementation.

Key message:

- She helps teams turn product intent into clear, stable, maintainable frontend work.

Additional text:

- 3-5 value statements.
- Each value should connect a behavior to a team outcome.
- Avoid raw technology categories such as "React, TypeScript, Tailwind".

Supporting elements:

- Value points with short explanations.
- Outcome-oriented labels:
  - product-minded frontend;
  - maintainable UI structure;
  - clear collaboration;
  - accessibility and detail quality;
  - responsible AI-assisted workflow.
- Optional quiet transition to commercial experience.

What the user should understand:

- The candidate can improve product clarity, implementation quality, and team communication.
- Her value is not limited to coding isolated UI pieces.

### 3. Commercial Experience

Purpose: ground the value promise in real professional context.

Primary user question:

> Is there real commercial experience behind these claims?

Main heading direction:

- A credible experience heading focused on practical frontend work.

Subtitle direction:

- A concise summary of commercial context, responsibilities, and production-minded work.

Key message:

- The developer has practical experience working with real product constraints, team processes, and production expectations.

Additional text:

- Role context and type of work.
- Responsibilities written as evidence of maturity.
- Collaboration signals.
- Technical context only where it supports credibility.

Supporting elements:

- Experience summary.
- Role or period metadata if available.
- Responsibility groups:
  - UI implementation;
  - component structure;
  - responsive behavior;
  - accessibility and polish;
  - collaboration with design/product/development context.
- Evidence rows or concise proof points.

What the user should understand:

- The candidate has worked in a commercial environment.
- The earlier value claims are supported by practical experience.
- A technical reviewer has enough reason to inspect further.

### 4. How I Approach Development

Purpose: reveal decision-making style and engineering maturity.

Primary user question:

> How does this developer work, and can the team trust her decisions?

Main heading direction:

- A heading about approach, judgment, and delivery quality.

Subtitle direction:

- A short statement that good frontend starts before writing components: understanding the goal, choosing simple structure, and shipping polished increments.

Key message:

- She works deliberately: understand first, structure carefully, build accessibly, and use AI as support while keeping ownership.

Additional text:

- 4-5 principles with practical explanations.
- Each principle should answer "how does this affect the team or product?"
- Avoid abstract slogans without behavior.

Supporting elements:

- Development principles:
  - understand the product goal before implementation;
  - design component boundaries for maintainability;
  - ship small, reviewable increments;
  - treat accessibility and responsive behavior as core work;
  - use AI to explore and accelerate, not to replace judgment.
- Quiet bridge to the AI section.

What the user should understand:

- The candidate has a mature process.
- She can make implementation decisions that are understandable, reviewable, and maintainable.
- AI use is controlled and professional.

### 5. Ask My AI

Purpose: provide personalized depth without turning the page into a long resume.

Primary user question:

> Can I quickly clarify what matters to me?

Main heading direction:

- A direct invitation to ask focused questions about experience, strengths, approach, and fit.

Subtitle direction:

- A short explanation that the AI helps explore the candidate's professional background and development approach based on approved portfolio content.

Key message:

- The AI section is a useful product feature for recruiter questions, not a decorative chatbot.

Additional text:

- Set expectations about what the AI can answer.
- Explain boundaries briefly: it should answer from portfolio-approved information and avoid pretending to know unavailable details.
- Keep helper text concise.

Supporting elements:

- Suggested recruiter questions.
- Input for a custom question.
- Submit CTA: `Ask my AI`.
- Response area.
- Limit or grounding note.
- Fallback state for unavailable AI.

What the user should understand:

- The candidate can present information interactively and responsibly.
- The AI feature can save time for recruiters and give technical reviewers focused answers.
- The site has a memorable product-like element without losing professionalism.

## Hero Concepts

The Hero is the most important emotional decision. It must create immediate clarity while making the portfolio feel considered, modern, and human.

### Concept 1: Engineering Clarity

Idea:

- Lead with frontend as structured engineering.
- The Hero frames the candidate as someone who brings order, maintainability, and reliable implementation to product interfaces.

Possible heading direction:

- "Frontend developer focused on clear, maintainable product interfaces."

Emotion:

- Trust, precision, technical confidence.

Difference:

- Strongest engineering signal.
- Less emotional, more direct and credible for technical reviewers.

Fit:

- Very strong fit. It supports the project identity and avoids generic self-promotion.

Risk:

- May feel slightly restrained if the supporting copy does not add a human note.

### Concept 2: Product-Minded Frontend

Idea:

- Lead with the connection between user value and frontend execution.
- The Hero presents frontend as a product discipline where clarity, usability, and maintainability meet.

Possible heading direction:

- "I build frontend that makes product ideas clear, usable, and maintainable."

Emotion:

- Confidence, relevance, practical value.

Difference:

- More recruiter-friendly than the purely engineering concept.
- It explains business value before technical detail.

Fit:

- Excellent fit. This concept best connects HR scanning needs with engineering credibility.

Risk:

- Needs concrete proof later so it does not sound like product-marketing language.

### Concept 3: Storytelling Through Work Style

Idea:

- Lead with how the developer thinks and works.
- The Hero creates a personal professional narrative: understand, structure, build, refine.

Possible heading direction:

- "I turn unclear interface problems into calm, structured frontend work."

Emotion:

- Curiosity, calm interest, human confidence.

Difference:

- More distinctive and narrative-driven.
- It positions the developer through process rather than title.

Fit:

- Strong fit if the portfolio wants to feel memorable and less like a conventional resume.

Risk:

- The role must remain visible so recruiters immediately understand the candidate is a Frontend Developer.

### Concept 4: Minimal Professional Signal

Idea:

- Lead with a short, precise statement and let restraint carry confidence.
- The Hero avoids explanation overload and relies on clean identity, photo, and one sharp value line.

Possible heading direction:

- "Frontend Developer. Product clarity. Maintainable interfaces."

Emotion:

- Calm, premium, controlled.

Difference:

- Most minimal and scannable.
- It feels confident because it does not over-explain.

Fit:

- Good fit for Powder Precision Tech and the desired premium feel.

Risk:

- Could feel too sparse unless the next section quickly expands the value.

### Concept 5: Human + AI Collaboration

Idea:

- Lead with the developer's ownership in an AI-assisted workflow.
- The Hero makes AI part of the professional story, but keeps the human engineer responsible for decisions.

Possible heading direction:

- "Frontend engineering with product judgment, AI support, and human ownership."

Emotion:

- Modernity, curiosity, controlled innovation.

Difference:

- Most distinctive because it previews the final AI section.
- It can make the portfolio feel current without becoming a gimmick.

Fit:

- Good fit, but only if AI is treated as one supporting signal rather than the whole identity.

Risk:

- Too much AI emphasis in the Hero may distract from core frontend credibility.

### Recommended Hero Direction

Use Concept 2 as the primary direction:

> Product-Minded Frontend

Reason:

- It answers recruiter needs quickly.
- It still gives technical reviewers a path into architecture, maintainability, and process.
- It fits the product vision: a portfolio that feels like a professional product, not a static CV.
- It leaves room for the final AI section to be a memorable continuation instead of dominating the first screen.

Concept 1 can provide the engineering vocabulary. Concept 3 can provide a more memorable phrase if the first draft feels too conventional.

## AI Section Strategy

The AI section should demonstrate usefulness, grounding, and professional restraint.

### Questions Recruiters May Want To Ask

- What role is she best suited for?
- What frontend strengths does she bring to a team?
- What commercial experience does she have?
- Why should we invite her to an interview?
- How does she collaborate with designers or product people?
- How does she handle unclear requirements?
- What makes her different from a typical junior or middle frontend candidate?
- What should I tell a hiring manager about her?
- What questions should we ask her in an interview?
- Is she more focused on UI implementation, architecture, or product thinking?

### Questions Technical Reviewers May Want To Ask

- How does she approach component architecture?
- How does she keep frontend code maintainable?
- How does she use TypeScript in UI work?
- How does she think about accessibility?
- How does she approach responsive layouts?
- How does she decide when to add an abstraction?
- How does she use AI without losing engineering control?
- What tradeoffs does she usually consider before implementation?

### Capabilities The AI Should Demonstrate

- Summarize the candidate for different audiences.
- Answer focused questions using approved portfolio content.
- Translate experience into recruiter-friendly talking points.
- Explain development approach with practical examples.
- Suggest interview angles based on the candidate's strengths.
- Clarify boundaries when information is unavailable.
- Keep answers concise and professional.

### AI Boundaries

- Do not invent employers, projects, metrics, or private details.
- Do not answer as if it has access to information outside the approved content source.
- Do not make exaggerated claims.
- Do not replace contact or interview flow.
- Do not become the main proof of competence; it is a way to explore existing proof.

## Storytelling

The page should tell one calm professional story:

> I am a Frontend Developer who builds clear, maintainable product interfaces. I bring value by connecting product intent with structured implementation. My commercial experience gives that value practical grounding. My development approach is deliberate, accessible, and maintainable. If you want details for your role or hiring context, ask my AI.

Story progression:

1. Hero: create attention and define professional positioning.
2. Team value: explain how the developer helps a team.
3. Commercial experience: prove that the value is grounded in real work.
4. Development approach: reveal how decisions are made.
5. Ask My AI: allow personalized exploration without adding more static sections.

The story should feel like a gradual trust build:

- identity;
- value;
- evidence;
- judgment;
- interactive clarification.

## Repetition Check

Potential repetition risks:

- "Maintainable frontend" can appear in Hero, team value, and approach.
- "Product clarity" can appear in Hero and team value.
- "AI-assisted workflow" can appear in team value, approach, and AI section.

Control rules:

- In Hero, use these ideas as positioning.
- In Team Value, connect them to outcomes for a team.
- In Commercial Experience, use them only as evidence.
- In Approach, explain the decision-making behavior behind them.
- In AI Section, use them as topics the user can explore.

## Content Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- a technology-stack section disguised as content strategy;
- a chronological resume narrative;
- vague claims such as "passionate", "creative", "hard-working";
- too many self-descriptive adjectives;
- repeating the same strengths with different wording;
- presenting AI as magic or a personality gimmick;
- making the page feel like a sales landing page.

## Recommendations Before Wireframes

Before moving to wireframes, decide:

- which Hero concept is approved;
- exact professional role label;
- whether the page uses first-person or third-person voice;
- confirmed commercial experience facts that may be shown publicly;
- final 3-5 team value points;
- final 4-5 development principles;
- approved AI grounding content and unavailable-information response rules;
- whether contact or interview flow is intentionally excluded from MVP.

Do not create new wireframes until the Hero direction and section content priorities are approved.

## Self-Review

Story interest:

- The story is stronger than a resume because it moves from identity to value, proof, thinking, and interactive depth.
- The final AI section adds a memorable product-like moment without changing the MVP section count.

Repetition:

- Some concepts repeat by design, but each section uses them for a different purpose.
- The document includes rules to keep repeated terms from becoming filler.

Water:

- Each section is tied to a concrete user question and expected understanding.
- Generic claims are discouraged unless supported by behavior or evidence.

Resume feeling:

- The strategy avoids chronological detail as the main structure.
- Commercial experience is included as proof, not as the page's organizing principle.

Conclusion:

- The content strategy is ready for copy drafting and then wireframe refinement, after the Hero direction is selected.
